Invasive Species of Vermont

Plants and animals that are introduced to and live in non-native ecosystems are called invasive species. As these species have the potential to outcompete native organisms and spread rapidly (especailly when their native predators are absent from the new enviornment), they can cause harm to the environment, as well as human health. Globalization has made it easy for human activity to lead to the spread of non-native orgnaisms. In New England, many invasive species have been discoved. Below is a series of invasive species that are present in Vermont. From left to right, they are: Emerald Ash Borer, Hemlock Woolly Adelgid, Commmon Buckthorn, Common Reed, & Elongate Hemlock Scale. More descriptions and images of Vermont invasive land species can be found here.

Certain areas in Vermont have been identified as having high biological significance/diversity. Conservation is recommended in these areas in order to ensure the continued survival of the native communities that make these areas important. The below map illustrates these biological hotspots. Invasive species can harm these areas. The map contains data from the Vermont Agency of Natural Resources (ANR) that locates spots where certain invasive species have been found.



Vermont Invasive Species Map


The yellow dots on the above map represent cases of Emerald Ash Borer. Emerald Ash Borer is an invasive beetle that originates from Asia. The beetle lays eggs in the bark of ash trees, and the larvae then tunnel within the tree's bark, usually resulting in tree death as food & water flow is negatively affected. Given that 5% of Vermonts trees are ash trees, the death of ash trees could cause large ecological harm.



Have you seen these signs before? This picture was taken on Dartmouth's campus! Emerald Ash Borer is also a threat in New Hampshire. There are efforts being made to reduce the spread. One of these methods is the use of insecticides on trees, as pictured here. Being able to spot, identify, and report Emerald Ash Borer can help with tracking and reducing the spread.
